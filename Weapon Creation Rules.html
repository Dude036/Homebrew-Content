Weapon Creation Rules

Have you ever wanted to add some flair to making a custom weapon? One that really feels like yours. To make something completely unique to your build. One that's less boring than just a longsword. That's what this ruleset is for.

Each section breaks down the basics for creating a weapon. Not all weapons are created equally, but there is some commonality. Here is the list of different types of weapons you can possibly create

  • Bladed Weapons
    • Swords
    • Polearms
    • Axes

  • Blunt Weapons
    • Clubs

  • Firearms

Material Properties

Certain Materials in the fantasy world have special properties. This section breaks down the general nature of the material, what it explicitly can and cannot do. Anything outside of the explicit description, should be discussed with your Game Master. Each material section contains a description of the material, if there are any special properties, what those are, and how those properties may interact with the outside world.

Firearms

Firearms are an immense leap forward in Technology. As such, incorporating them into your games can bring a lot of scrutiny as to their function, creation, and features. This section attempts to explain their creation and general function. I leave the features and facets to your imagination.

The following is the creation process of a firearm from start to finish. All steps can be done completely seperate. Your Gun can be disassembled and rebuilt as part of the clean procedure, so you're free to rebuild your Gun as you see fit.

  1. Barrel Casting
  2. Stock Casting/Whittling
  3. Ammo Tinkering
  4. Receiver Tinkering
  5. Final Assembly

For the steps that have you roll on a Success table, you calculate the success with the following formula:

Success = 1d100 + Gunslinger Level + Tool Proficiency + Dexterity + Success Modifer
Barrel Casting
Requirements: Active Forge + Smith's Tools + Tinker's Tools

Casting is the process of forging material and placing the molten material into a cast, which is then used to create the barrel. This part needs to be the most hardy of the firearm, as it's the piece that needs to withstand the most abuse, and remain true. There are several different types of materials that can be used to create a Barrel, each with different properties.

When crafting a Barrel, you'll need an active forge for a few hours. To begin creating a barrel, choose your material, spend the requisite time building the barrel, and finally roll on the success table. At the end of this process, you have a part that's worthy of being attached to a gun.

  • Pistol: 2 Hours + 2 lbs of Material
  • Rifle & Shotgun: 3 Hours + 5 lbs of Material
  • Sniper: 4 Hours + 8 lbs of Material
Note: 1 Hour is allotted to prep work during a long rest.

Stock Casting/Whittling
Requirements: Active Forge + Smith's Tools or Woodworking Tools + Tinker's Tools

You can create a stock from the two major materials. Woods and Metals. Wood offers a softer Stock, and more suitable for extended use. It's also something you that's easier to create while in the wild. Metals, on the other hand, offer a more rigid experience, and thus are more effective with weapons that are fired continuously. Metal is also more durable, and less likely to break upon impact.

When creating a Stock, you follow a similar pattern to creating a barrel. Choose a Material, Spend the needed time, then roll on the success table.

Build Time and Material Cost by Weapon Type:

  • Pistol: 1 Hours + 1 lbs of Material
  • Rifle & Shotgun: 3 Hours + 2 lbs of Material
  • Sniper: 6 Hours + 3 lbs of Material
Note: 1 Hour is allotted to prep work during a long rest.

Ammo Tinkering
Requirements: Tinker's Tools

This is the stage where you decide which type of Ammo is going to be used, and which the gun will be capable of firing. There are a few things to note when deciding Ammo, you are not restricting the type of firearm you can make. A pistol cannot fire a Long Distance Sniper round. This process also dictates how much metal is required to craft a bullet for your weapon.

Since this step is a decision, and not based on your crafting capability, there's no rolling associated with this step. You simply choose the Loading Mechanism and the Bullet Caliber that's right for you, and spend the time necessary to build the Casing and Magazine.

Build Time by Weapon Type:

  • Pistol: 2 Hours
  • Rifle & Shotgun: 3 Hours
  • Sniper: 5 Hours
Note: 1 Hour is allotted to prep work during a long rest.

Receiver Tinkering
Requirements: Tinker's Tools

This is where the magic happens. Tinkering with delicate yet durable parts is the core of who a gunslinger is. This step is probably the most complicated of all the steps, and requires a lot of talent and instruction to do properly. Below are the build times and material costs for combining a receiver.

As with previous steps, begin with collecting the necessary materials, spend the requisite time building, and finally roll on the Success table.

  • Pistol: 5 Hours + 2 lbs of Material
  • Rifle & Shotgun: 7 Hours + 5 lbs of Material
  • Sniper: 9 Hours + 8 lbs of Material
Note: 1 Hour is allotted to prep work during a long rest.

Final Assembly
Requirements: Tinker's Tools

Final Assembly is the part you've been looking forward to. After all, it's when you finally get your Gun to a usable state. To begin final assembly, ensure all of your parts are compatible. Finally, assemble them together with your Tinker's Tools. You can also dismantle them with this step, to rebuild a new weapon with old parts.

  • Pistol: 20 Minutes
  • Rifle & Shotgun: 30 Minutes
  • Sniper: 45 Minutes
Note: 1 Hour is allotted to prep work during a long rest.
